Applied Example ‚Äî Physics

See concrete examples in action with Newton's Second Law.

F = m ¬∑ a
The net force on an object equals its mass times its acceleration.
Greater force ‚Üí greater acceleration ¬∑ Greater mass ‚Üí less acceleration.
1

Your teacher showed that pushing an empty toy cart vs. a loaded toy cart with the same force makes the empty one accelerate faster ‚Äî same F, less m, more a.

2

Kicking a soccer ball vs. a bowling ball ‚Äî your leg applies roughly the same force, but the soccer ball flies while the bowling ball barely moves. Greater mass, less acceleration!

3

"Heavier things are harder to speed up! That's why a truck takes longer to start moving from a stop sign compared to a small car."

‚úÖ Start by collecting the examples already given in class ‚Äî they're your foundation.
‚ùå Not quite. The first step is to collect and note the examples your teachers and peers share in class.

2. Why should you explain examples to a friend?

‚úÖ Teaching others forces you to organize your thoughts and exposes any gaps in your understanding.
‚ùå The real reason is that explaining to someone else reinforces the concept and helps you find gaps.

‚ùå Since a = F/m, doubling m while keeping F the same gives you half the acceleration.
